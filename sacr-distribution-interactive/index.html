Exploring Changes in Distribution and Count of Sandhill Cranes in the Eastern Population over 50 years

Data visualization: Dorn Moore, Jim Cunningham, & Randy Garcia
Research Team: Anne Lacy, Jeb Barzen, Dorn Moore, Kristin Norris

In 2015, researchers at the International Crane Foundation published a paper in the Journal of Field Ornithology examining the changes in the number and distribution of Sandhill cranes in the Eastern Population (download the original article). The Eastern Population of Sandhill Cranes in the United States is located in the Mississippi and Atlantic flyways grew dramatically between 1966 and 2013. The research team used data from annual bird surveys to help grasp the substantial changes in the number of Sandhill cranes in the population from 1966 through 2013.

For winter analysis, the project team used data from the National Audubon Society’s Christmas Bird Count. Volunteers - citizen scientists - count birds as part of Christmas Bird Count (CBC) between December 14th and January 5th each year. These data are used by researchers to help understand changes in bird populations. Our analysis of CBC data revealed both major increases in the number of Sandhills in the Eastern Population but also significant changes in the timing and distribution of Sandhills during their winter migration.

The majority of Sandhill cranes within the Eastern Population spend their summer/breeding months in or north of Michigan, Wisconsin, and Minnesota. The data visualization below helps explore both the changes in numbers of cranes from 1966-2013 but also the changes in the distribution of the cranes during the Christmas Bird Count. It is easy to see the northward shift in the distribution of Sandhills and also how they spread to areas along the Gulf Coast and the Eastern seaboard.

Although these data do not necessarily reflect the ultimate wintering area for Sandhills in the region, they do reflect changes in the areas that will still support cranes as winter arrives. Sandhills are hardy birds; many of them will wait to migrate until open water for roosting and drinking has frozen over completely. Sandhills are able to postpone their migration until later than the Christmas Bird Count each year. The research team documented a significant northern shift in the average latitude of the Eastern Population at the Christmas Bird Count each year. The chart below shows the northward shift and the broadening range of latitudes where Sandhills are staging or wintering each year.

Average Latitude of Sandhill Cranes Recorded in Christmas Bird Count (1966 - 2013)
The changes in the average latitude for Sandhills in late December and early January reflect changes in the species population, changing habitat and food sources, and a change in the climate during the same period. The northern trend of the average latitude (orange line) over time is accompanied by an envelope (blue area) showing the core of the population's spread across different latitudes.
